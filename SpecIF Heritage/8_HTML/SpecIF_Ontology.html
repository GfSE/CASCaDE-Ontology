Introduction

The SpecIF Ontology (Vocabulary) collects terms used in the domain of systems engineering and requirements management. Agreed-upon terms help both collaborators to communicate more clearly and systems to match data fields automatically. SpecIF adopts terms and their definitions from other initiatives, for example from the Dublin Core Metadata Initiative (DCMI), the Open Services for Lifecycle Collaboration (OSLC), the International Requirements Engineering Board (IREB) and the ProSTEP iViP association to be used in ReqIF data.

All terms carry a namespace, which is the first part separated with ':' or '.' ― they are thus easily recognized in text and data fields. For display, systems may choose to replace the ontology terms by domain or language specific words. This collection itself is structured according to SpecIF using the ontology defined here. For example, the term descriptions are recognized as such, because they are tagged with 'dcterms:description'.

The terms have a lifecycle status as follows:

  • experimental: The term is being used in first projects, but has not yet any normative maturity.
  • submitted: The term has been submitted for adoption.
  • released: The term has been adopted for use with SpecIF and is preferred among equivalent terms.
  • equivalent: The term has been adopted for use with SpecIF, but the released term is preferred.
  • deprecated: The term shall not any more be used with SpecIF; use a released term.

The Specification Interchange Facility (SpecIF) is the result of an initiative by the Gesellschaft für Systems Engineering (GfSE), the German chapter of The International Council on Systems Engineering (INCOSE).

Resource Class Terms

A resource represents an artefact, e.g. a model element or a requirement. The terms listed in this chapter may be used to distinguish different classes (types) of artefacts constituting a system specification. A resource may serve as subject or as object in a subject-predicate-object statement. Each resource class may define an individual set of properties for carrying the information 'payload'.

A resource class corresponds to a 'SPEC-OBJECT-TYPE' and a resource is a 'SPEC-OBJECT' in the Requirements Interchange Format (ReqIF). In ArchiMate® 3.0, a resource is an Element.

IREB:Requirement

A 'Requirement' is a singular documented physical and functional need that a particular design, product or process must be able to perform. (source: Wikipedia)

Definition:

  1. A condition or capability needed by a user to solve a problem or achieve an objective.
  2. A condition or capability that must be met or possessed by a system or system component to satisfy a contract, standard, specification, or other formally imposed documents.
  3. A documented representation of a condition or capability as in (1) or (2).

Note: The definition above is the classic one from IEEE Std 610.12 of 1990. Alternatively, we also give a more modern definition:

  1. A need perceived by a stakeholder.
  2. A capability or property that a system shall have.
  3. A documented representation of a need, capability or property.

ArchiMate:DataObject

Definition: A data object represents data structured for automated processing.

A data object should be a self-contained piece of information with a clear meaning to the business, not just to the application level. Typical examples of data objects are a customer record, a client database, or an insurance claim.

The ArchiMate language in general focuses on the modeling of types, not instances, since this is the most relevant at the Enterprise Architecture level of description. Hence a data object typically models an object type (cf. a UML class) of which multiple instances may exist in operational applications. An important exception is when a data object is used to model a data collection such as a database, of which only one instance exists.

An application function or process can operate on data objects. A data object may be communicated via interactions and used or produced by application services. A data object can be accessed by an application function, application interaction, or application service. A data object may realize a business object, and may be realized by an artifact. A data object may have association, specialization, aggregation, or composition relationships with other data objects. The name of a data object should preferably be a noun.

ArchiMate:BusinessObject

Definition: A business object represents a concept used within a particular business domain.

The ArchiMate language in general focuses on the modeling of types, not instances, since this is the most relevant at the Enterprise Architecture level of description. Hence a business object typically models an object type (cf. a UML class) of which multiple instances may exist in operations. Only occasionally, business objects represent actual instances of information produced and consumed by behavior elements such as business processes. This is in particular the case for singleton types; i.e., types that have only one instance.

A wide variety of types of business objects can be defined. Business objects are passive in the sense that they do not trigger or perform processes. A business object could be used to represent information assets that are relevant from a business point of view and can be realized by data objects.

Business objects may be accessed (e.g., in the case of information objects, they may be created, read, written) by a business process, function, business interaction, business event, or business service. A business object may have association, specialization, aggregation, or composition relationships with other business objects. A business object may be realized by a representation or by a data object (or both). The name of a business object should preferably be a noun.

Statement Class Terms

A statement represents a logic affinity or association between two resources, i.e. system specification artefacts. The terms listed in this chapter may be used as predicates to distinguish different classes (types) of statements. Each statement class may define an individual set of properties for carrying the information 'payload'.

In SpecIF, a statement is first order predicate logic. For each predicate (statement class), a list of eligible resource classes may be defined for subjects and objects, respectively. For example, the predicate oslc_rm:satisfies is usually confined to FMC:Actor and FMC:State as subject and IREB:Requirement as object.

A statement consisting of subject-predicate-object (where a resource may serve as subject or object) can be directly mapped to a ReqIF relation, a RDF triple in OSLC or 'linked data' in general.

A statement class corresponds to a 'SPEC-RELATION-TYPE' and a statement is a 'SPEC-RELATION' in the Requirements Interchange Format (ReqIF). In ArchiMate® 3.0, a statement is a Relationship.

SpecIF:OrdinalMark

A string to determine the ordering of elements. Rules:

  • For ordering, only alphanumeric characters are permissible. Uppercase or lowerCase has no effect; thus, 'F' is equivalent to 'f'.
  • The ordering at character level is alphanumeric, i.e. '1' &lt; '7' &lt; 'D' &lt; 'X'.
  • Single character substrings &lt; two-character substrings &lt; substrings with more characters, thus '9' &lt; '12' &lt; '114'.
  • The ordinal mark may be separated by non-alphanumeric characters. All separators are equivalent; e.g. '-' is equivalent to ';' or '/'.
  • Each alphanumeric substring is used for ordering on its hierarchical level; the leftmost substring is most significant for the overall ordering.
  • For example '1' &lt; '1.2' &lt; '1.Y' &lt; '1.1a' &lt; '1-1b' &lt; '1-1C' &lt; '2'.

A regular expression for extracting the substrings of an ordinal mark is &#47;([0-9a-z]+)[^0-9a-z]{0,1}&#47;gi.
